J-20 Mighty Dragon

The Chengdu J‑20 Mighty Dragon is a Chinese fifth gen stealth multirole fighter built by Chengdu Aircraft Industry Corporation. Although the plane first flew in 2011 the jet wasn’t introduced into PLAAF operations until March 2017, with actual combat readiness not being until later in 2018. The J-20 is the third ever fifth generation fighter jet to enter service after the US made F-22 and F-35. Like the F-22 the J-20 is able to break the sound barrier without using afterburners which is a feature that few fighter jets are able to accomplish. An uncommon feature of the J-20 is no internal autocannon not allowing for a gun fight if required. Although missile combat is the most common thing, countries like America are scared to remove it due to what happened in Vietnam when they originally did and they’re still useful for things such as air to ground and other capabilities.

J-10 Vigorous Dragon

The Chengdu J‑10 Vigorous Dragon is a Chinese fourth-generation multirole fighter built by Chengdu Aircraft Industry Corporation. It emerged as the winner of China’s No. 10 Project in the 1980s. It’s maiden flight was in March 1998 with it going through lots of testing before entering full service close to six years later in December of 2003. This was one of China’s first full domestically designed and manufactured in China. Recent usage in Pakistan will gain more popularity for Chinese vehicles in the export market.

J-15 Flying Shark

The J‑15 is a Chinese naval multirole fighter developed by Shenyang Aircraft Corporation under the Flying Shark program. It comes from an incomplete Soviet Su‑33 prototype acquired from Ukraine that China used to reverse engineer their J-15. Shenyang had previously made the J-11B from reverse engineering the Su-27’s they were domestically producing under the name J-11A. This previous experience made them suitable to reverse engineer another fighter jet and make a similar one for the Chinese navy. The J‑15 program officially began in 2006. Six years later the jet landed aboard China’s first carrier, Liaoning, successfully on November 25, 2012 marking China's first naval fighter arresting cable landing. Finally in 2013 operational service marking the first Chinese naval fighter. Later variants made allowed them to takeoff with a catapult and allowed them to work on modern Chinese carriers that didn’t have a ski jump for takeoff.
