Stories

F-16’s First Flight:

Phil Oestricher was completing a high speed taxi test where they were supposed to accelerate up to 240km/h on the runway and then stop. This was to test the prototype airframe but whilst accelerating Phil was unable to get the plane under control causing the plane to start veering off the runway on its side. In a snap decision Phil lifted up the nose and took off for its first accidental flight on January 20 1974. This unexpected flight happened due to the new features on the aircraft that the test pilot wasn’t used and lucky got it back to safety after a six minute flight. A video of the occurrence is here.

F-16 Dodges 6 SAMs:

On January 19, 1991, during the start of Operation Desert Storm, F‑16 pilot Major Emmett “Stroke 3” Tullia flew over heavily defended Baghdad to strike an oil refinery. After hitting the target, he was met with a barrage of six Iraqi surface-to-air missiles (SAMs). Despite his flares and chaff failing to deploy, Tullia used intense high‑G maneuvers, sharp turns, barrel rolls, dives to dodge each one. At one point he even heard a missile’s motor roaring as it zipped by. For around seven stressful minutes he wriggled through the missile onslaught purely through flying skill, not countermeasures, and managed to return safely, earning him the Distinguished Flying Cross. F-15 Eagle

The F‑15 Eagle is an American twin‑engine air superiority fighter built by McDonnell Douglas. It won the USAF’s F‑X competition, aiming to deliver high‑performance aerial dominance rather than lightweight multirole flexibility. The first prototype flew on July 27, 1972 at Edwards AFB. The trainer variant flew in July 1973. The first F‑15B was delivered in November 1974, and the aircraft entered operational service in September 1975 (combat squadron deliveries began January 1976). The F-15 has the highest air to air kill ratio at 104-0.

During a training exercise in Israel in 1983 a F-15 collided with an A-4 skyhawk causing a wing of the F-15 to come off. F-22 Raptor

The F‑22 Raptor is a U.S. Air Force fifth‑generation stealth air superiority fighter developed by Lockheed Martin in partnership with Boeing and powered by Pratt & Whitney engines. It traces its roots to the Advanced Tactical Fighter (ATF) competition of the 1980s, which pitted the YF‑22 against the YF‑23. Although the YF‑23 was stealthier and faster, the YF‑22 was more maneuverable, flown more extensively in testing, and came in at lower risk and cost—ultimately winning the contract on April 23, 1991. Despite flying in 1997, the F‑22 wasn’t declared operational until December 15, 2005. Deliveries began in late 2002, and the last of 195 aircraft rolled out in May 2012.

F/A-18 Super Hornet

The F/A‑18E/F Super Hornet is an American carrier‑based multirole fighter built by McDonnell Douglas. It traces its lineage to the Northrop YF‑17 “Cobra” a competitor in the Air Force’s Lightweight Fighter program which, after losing to the YF‑16, was scaled up into the original F/A‑18 Hornet for the Navy. The Super Hornet made its maiden flight on November 29, 1995, and entered operational service with the U.S. Navy in 1999. Although it only replaced the Grumman F‑14 Tomcat which retired fully in 2006, the Super Hornet has gone on to become the backbone of U.S carrier aviation and remains in service and combat operations into the 2020s. This fighter jet will be one of the most well known and iconic aircraft due to Top Gun: Maverick.
